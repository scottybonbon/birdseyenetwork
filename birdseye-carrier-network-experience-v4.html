02 · Why this isn't new

We've got eyes.
We've got your back.

Picture the guard in the booth. A truck rolls in with a cracked windshield or a flat tire. What does he do? He calls the carrier and tells them. It's nowhere in the service agreement. It's just what you do when you're keeping an eye out.

Birdseye is an extension of our customer's team. We already have thousands of these conversations a day, keeping people up to speed on what we see. The Network doesn't invent a new behavior. It takes the one that already works and scales it.

A guard remembers the company he helped, and it pays off years later. We can do that a hundred thousand times a month, on purpose.

"It's like seeing someone on the road with a broken headlight and flashing your lights. You're just keeping an eye out."

    07 · The real constraint

    The blocker isn't the tech.
    It's belief.

    The tech team is already automating the detection, the email, the article-pull. Engineering is not what stands between us and this.

    The one real constraint is internal buy-in: the belief that this is worth doing on purpose. Leadership owns the vision and gives the mandate. Then someone great at execution runs it.

    The reason it stalls is that it sounds too grand, too big. Where do you even start? So we make it small.

    Start humble. Prove it. Then scale.
    01Leadership explains the vision at a town hall: here's what this is, and why.
    02Define a manageable pilot and pick a few sites.
    03Establish the communication and escalation process.
    04Account coordinators confirm carriers find the alerts useful.
    05Collect the right contacts, with permission.
    06Pass qualified accounts to the BDR team to map.
    07Meet twice weekly while it gets off the ground.
    08Measure the response before scaling.

    10 · The thing that gates it all

    Trust is the mechanism.

    The moment this feels sales-driven or surveillant, the trust we've built collapses. These are the questions we resolve before we scale, not after.

    01
    Permission to use

    Can we use the authorized-arrival lists our customers already give us, for outreach, not just operations? Get this in writing.

    02
    Ongoing consent

    Have carriers agreed to keep hearing from us? Confirmed on the courtesy call, easy to stop anytime.

    03
    Our customer's control

    Messages go out in their name and at their facility. They decide what's shared and can switch it off.

    04
    Facts, never fault

    Observations describe what was visible at entry. Careful wording keeps us helpful and limits liability.

    05
    Human review

    Where does an automated assessment need a person to sign off before it's sent? Draw that line clearly.

    06
    Service vs. marketing

    A bright line between the courtesy and the sale. Cross it too early and the whole thing reads as a pitch.

    This is why we lead with the digest: it's first-party (our customer, their yard), so we prove value while counsel clears the carrier side. The opportunity is real, but trust is what makes it work.
